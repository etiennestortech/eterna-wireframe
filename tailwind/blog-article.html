What is Hermetic Preservation? Understanding the Capsule Pyrale

A clear, honest explanation of what the Capsule Pyrale is, how nitrogen gas works to preserve the body naturally, and why this patented technology represents something genuinely new — for families seeking a dignified, eco-conscious alternative to embalming and cremation.

[Photo]

Marie-Claude Beaumont

Contributing Writer, Eterna

February 18, 2026 8 min read
[Image]

Until recently, most families faced a choice with very little middle ground: burial or cremation. The first involves embalming — a process that introduces formaldehyde and other chemicals into the body, then into the earth. The second involves high-temperature combustion. Both have served their purpose for generations. But a growing number of families are asking whether there is another way — one that is gentler, cleaner, and more aligned with the values they held in life.

The Capsule Pyrale is that other way. Developed over more than a decade of research by Pierre Béliveau in Granby, Québec, it is an hermetic aluminum capsule that preserves the body naturally, using inert nitrogen gas, for an estimated 60 to 100 years — with no chemicals, no embalming, and no environmental contamination of any kind.

What Does "Hermetic" Mean?

The word "hermetic" comes from the Greek and means perfectly sealed — airtight to the point of being impermeable. A hermetic container admits no air, no moisture, and no external matter. It is the same principle used to preserve food, protect sensitive instruments, and seal aircraft components.

In the context of the Capsule Pyrale, hermetic sealing means that once the cover is locked with a specialized sealing key and the high-density industrial rubber joints are engaged, nothing enters or exits the capsule. No odors escape. No air enters. The interior environment — once filled with inert nitrogen — remains stable and controlled for the duration of the preservation period.

Why Nitrogen? The Science, Simply Explained

Nitrogen is not an exotic chemical. It makes up approximately 78% of the air we breathe every day. It is inert — meaning it does not react with other substances under normal conditions. It is colorless, odorless, and entirely non-toxic.

The reason nitrogen preserves the body comes down to oxygen deprivation. The biological processes that cause decomposition — the breakdown of tissue by bacteria and enzymes — require oxygen to function. Without oxygen, those processes slow dramatically and, in the sealed environment of the Capsule Pyrale, effectively cease. The body dries gently rather than decomposing. No chemicals are introduced. No toxic byproducts are released.

"We believe the last gesture we offer to our loved ones should reflect their true worth. Not a chemical process. Not a rushed farewell. A serene, dignified presence — preserved with care."
— Pierre Béliveau, Founder of Eterna

How It Differs From Embalming

Traditional embalming involves draining the body's fluids and replacing them with formaldehyde-based solutions. Formaldehyde is a known carcinogen. It temporarily delays visible decomposition, typically for a period of days to a few weeks, and is used primarily to enable a viewing before burial or cremation.

The Capsule Pyrale achieves a fundamentally different result through an entirely different mechanism. No fluids are removed. No chemicals are introduced. The body is placed inside as it is, the capsule is sealed, and inert nitrogen does the rest. The preservation period — 60 to 100 years — is not a cosmetic delay. It is genuine, long-term stabilization of the body in a natural state.

For families who feel strongly about what enters the body — whether for environmental reasons, religious convictions, or simply a personal belief in a gentler process — this distinction matters enormously.

How It Differs From Cremation

Cremation reduces the body to ash through combustion at temperatures between 760°C and 982°C. It is faster, requires less physical space, and avoids the permanence of a burial plot. But it also consumes significant energy, produces CO₂ and other emissions, and — most importantly — it is irreversible. Once cremated, the body cannot be viewed again.

The Capsule Pyrale preserves the body intact. Families who choose it can hold a viewing, can take the time to gather loved ones from near and far, and can say goodbye over days rather than hours. For families who have experienced the acute pressure of an immediate, rushed farewell, this extended time is not a luxury. It is a profound difference in how grief unfolds.

Environmental Benefits

The environmental case for the Capsule Pyrale rests on several concrete facts. The main structure is made of 100% recyclable aluminum — one of the most recoverable materials in existence. The inert nitrogen gas produces no toxic byproduct and leaves no chemical trace in the surrounding environment. Unlike traditional burial, there is no embalming fluid leaching into the groundwater. Unlike cremation, there are no carbon emissions from the process itself.

Pierre Béliveau's original motivation was, in part, environmental. As a boy, he was troubled by the idea of burial in contact with rising groundwater. The Capsule Pyrale is, in part, his answer to that concern — a sealed, self-contained system that honors the body without leaving a toxic mark on the earth it returns to.

The Emotional Dimension

Technology is not the only reason families choose the Capsule Pyrale. There is a human dimension that is harder to quantify but no less real.

The capsule's polycarbonate cover is transparent. Family members can be present with their loved one — see them, sit near them — in a serene and dignified setting, without odor, without distress. The integrated LED lighting can be softened to whatever tone the family chooses. An engraving on the capsule — a name, a date, a word that mattered — can mark the person it holds.

For those who have lost someone suddenly, or whose family is scattered across the country or the world, the ability to extend the farewell by even a few days can be transformative. Grief is not linear. It does not fit inside a 48-hour window. The Capsule Pyrale was built, in part, with that understanding.

Because saying goodbye is never simple. And because the farewell should not be rushed.
